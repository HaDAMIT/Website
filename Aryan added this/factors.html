Contributing Factors 

The 'Life Below Water' goal addresses various contributing factors that pose challenges to the conservation and sustainable use of oceans, seas, and marine resources. Here are some key factors and examples:






Overfishing

Unsustainable fishing methods have resulted in the destruction of marine ecosystems and the depletion of fish populations. The FAO of the United Nations estimates that 33% of the world's fish stocks are being fished at levels that are not sustainable. Fisheries collapse, biodiversity loss, and adverse effects on coastal populations who depend on fishing for a living are all possible outcomes of overfishing.

Marine Pollution

Marine ecosystems are seriously threatened by pollution from a variety of sources, such as plastics, chemicals, oil spills, and untreated wastewater. Each year, the oceans get an estimated 8 million tonnes of plastic waste. Ingestion, entanglement, and habitat damage are all effects of plastic pollution that cause harm to marine life. For instance, sea turtles and seabirds frequently mistake garbage made of plastic for food, which causes harm and even death. Long-term ecological harm is done when oil spills, like the Deepwater Horizon accident in 2010, have severe consequences on marine ecosystems and animals.

Habitat Destruction

Coastal development, destructive fishing practices, and climate change impacts contribute to the degradation and loss of crucial marine habitats. Coral reefs, known as the rainforests of the sea, are particularly vulnerable. It is estimated that 30% of coral reefs are already severely degraded, and if current trends continue, 90% of the world's reefs could be threatened by 2030. Destruction of coral reefs disrupts marine biodiversity, reduces fish populations, and weakens coastal protection against storms and erosion.



Coral Bleaching

Rising ocean temperatures and climate change have caused widespread coral bleaching events. About 75% of the world's coral reefs are at risk, with the Great Barrier Reef experiencing severe bleaching events in recent years. If not addressed, the loss of coral reefs could have devastating effects on marine biodiversity and coastal communities.
